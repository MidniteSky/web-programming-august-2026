# Where next — carrying on after the course

You've just built and deployed a real web application from nothing. The single most
important thing now is to **keep going while it's fresh** — a little and often beats
a heroic weekend and then silence. Here's an opinionated, curated path. You don't
need all of it; pick a lane and start.

## Keep the fundamentals growing

- **[MDN Web Docs](https://developer.mozilla.org)** — the reference we used all week,
  and the one you'll use forever. Beyond the reference pages, **[MDN
  Learn](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Learn)** is a free, structured
  curriculum that covers the same ground you did and then some, at your own pace.
  When you're stuck on anything, "mdn" + the thing is still the best search you can do.
- **[javascript.info](https://javascript.info)** — "The Modern JavaScript Tutorial".
  Thorough, clear, free, and goes deeper than a five-day course ever could. If one
  resource becomes your JavaScript home after this, make it this one.

## Practise the muscle memory (little games)

You learned layout by hand this week; these make the practice fun:

- **[Flexbox Froggy](https://flexboxfroggy.com)** — learn Flexbox by moving frogs
  onto lily pads. Twenty minutes, genuinely enjoyable, and it'll cement Day 2.
- **[Grid Garden](https://cssgridgarden.com)** — the same idea for CSS Grid: water
  your carrots with grid properties.
- Both are free, short, and worth doing more than once.

## When you're ready for a proper syllabus

- **[The Odin Project](https://www.theodinproject.com)** — a free, full,
  project-based curriculum that takes you from roughly where you are now toward
  job-ready, in a sensible order. It's a big commitment, but it's the real thing, and
  it's genuinely free. If you want a structured "what do I learn and in what order"
  answer, this is our top recommendation.
- Curious about a framework? **Don't rush it** — but when you're ready, start with
  the *official* tutorials (React's and Vue's own docs are excellent). You'll pick it
  up far faster now, because you understand what the DOM does underneath. And read
  the honest "when do you actually need one" section in Day 5's demo 10 first.

## Build these three next (they grow your Member Manager)

The best way to keep learning is to build. Each of these extends what you already
have, one new idea at a time:

1. **A second list, your own.** Clone the Member Manager into a "Book tracker",
   "Recipe box" or "Gig list" — same CRUD, your data shape. Renaming and re-theming
   an app you understand teaches you more than you'd expect, and proves the pattern
   is yours.
2. **Sort and filter, properly.** Add clickable column headers that sort the table,
   and a "show active only" toggle. All within this week's toolkit (`sort`, `filter`,
   event listeners) — no new tools, just deeper fluency.
3. **A details page.** Make each member's name a link to a `member.html?id=3` page
   that fetches and shows just that one member. This introduces reading the URL and
   `GET /members/3` — a real step toward how multi-page apps actually work.

When those feel comfortable, the natural next frontiers are a **real back end** (so
your deployed site's data works for everyone, not just on your laptop) and a
**framework** (once an app gets big enough to want one). Both are well-signposted in
MDN Learn and The Odin Project.

## A few honest words

- **You will forget things.** That's normal — nobody holds all of this in their head.
  Knowing *what exists* and being able to look it up is the actual skill.
- **Finishing is a skill of its own.** A small thing you completed and deployed beats
  a huge thing you abandoned. You just finished and deployed something — do it again,
  smaller and often.
- **Read other people's code.** Now you can. Open the demos again, read the Bootstrap
  docs' examples, poke at pages with DevTools. Curiosity is the whole job.

You've got the foundations. Go build something you'll want to show people.
